Understanding Italian word order is your key to building natural-sounding sentences. While Italian follows many similar patterns to English, it also has some flexible rules that can trip up learners. Let's master these patterns together!

## Basic Sentence Structure: SVO

Like English, Italian typically follows Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) order:

| English | Italian |
|---------|---------|
| I love you | (Io) amo te / Ti amo |
| Marco reads a book | Marco legge un libro |
| We cook dinner | (Noi) cuciniamo la cena |

<CultureTip title="Dropping Subjects" flag="üáÆüáπ">
Italian often drops subject pronouns because verb endings indicate who is doing the action. "Amo" already means "I love" - adding "io" is optional emphasis. This is one of the biggest differences from English!
</CultureTip>

## Where Adjectives Go

This is one of the trickiest parts of Italian word order. Most adjectives come AFTER the noun (opposite of English):

### Adjectives That Follow the Noun (Most Common)

| Type | English | Italian |
|------|---------|---------|
| Color | a red rose | una rosa **rossa** |
| Nationality | an Italian man | un uomo **italiano** |
| Shape | a round table | un tavolo **rotondo** |
| Physical quality | blue eyes | occhi **blu** |

### Adjectives That Precede the Noun (BANGS)

Some common adjectives come BEFORE the noun. Remember **BANGS**:

- **N**umber: primo, secondo, ultimo
- **G**oodness: buono, cattivo, bravo
- **S**ize: grande, piccolo, lungo, corto

|---------|---------|
| a beautiful woman | una **bella** donna |
| a good idea | una **buona** idea |

### Meaning Changes with Position

Some adjectives change meaning based on position:

| Before Noun | After Noun |
|-------------|------------|
| un **grande** uomo (a great man) | un uomo **grande** (a big/tall man) |
| un **vecchio** amico (an old/longtime friend) | un amico **vecchio** (an elderly friend) |
| una **povera** donna (a poor/pitiful woman) | una donna **povera** (a poor/not rich woman) |
| un **caro** amico (a dear friend) | un regalo **caro** (an expensive gift) |

<CultureTip title="When in Doubt" flag="üáÆüáπ">

</CultureTip>

## Object Pronouns: Before the Verb

Unlike English, Italian object pronouns come BEFORE the conjugated verb:

| English | Italian |
|---------|---------|
| I love **you** | **Ti** amo |
| I see **him** | **Lo** vedo |
| She loves **me** | **Mi** ama |
| We call **them** | **Li** chiamiamo |

### Direct Object Pronouns

| Pronoun | Italian | Example |
|---------|---------|---------|
| me | mi | Mi ami? (Do you love me?) |
| you | ti | Ti chiamo stasera (I'll call you tonight) |
| him/it | lo | Lo vedo domani (I see him tomorrow) |
| her/it | la | La aspetto (I'm waiting for her) |
| us | ci | Ci invitano (They invite us) |
| you (pl.) | vi | Vi ringrazio (I thank you all) |
| them (m.) | li | Li conosco (I know them) |
| them (f.) | le | Le vedo spesso (I see them often) |

## Pronouns with Infinitives

When a verb is followed by an infinitive, pronouns can attach to the end of the infinitive:

| Form 1 | Form 2 | Meaning |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Ti voglio vedere | Voglio vederti | I want to see you |
| Ti posso chiamare | Posso chiamarti | I can call you |
| Mi vuoi aiutare? | Vuoi aiutarmi? | Do you want to help me? |

Both forms are correct - use whichever feels more natural!

## Question Word Order

Italian questions often just use rising intonation (like English "You're coming?"):

| Statement | Question |
|-----------|----------|
| Vieni stasera. | Vieni stasera? |
| Ti piace. | Ti piace? |
| Sei stanco. | Sei stanco? |

## Negation Placement

"Non" comes directly before the verb (and any pronouns):

| Affirmative | Negative |
|-------------|----------|
| Ti amo | **Non** ti amo |
| Lo so | **Non** lo so |
| Capisco | **Non** capisco |
| Ci vado | **Non** ci vado |

## Emphasis and Flexibility

Italian word order can be rearranged for emphasis. The stressed element often comes last:

| Neutral | Emphasis on |
|---------|-------------|
| Io amo te | You (Te amo io) |
| Marco ha comprato i fiori | Marco (I fiori li ha comprati Marco) |
| Partiamo domani | Tomorrow (Domani partiamo) |

<CultureTip title="Expressive Italian" flag="üáÆüáπ">
Italians love to play with word order for dramatic effect. Moving elements around can add emotion, surprise, or emphasis. Listen to native speakers and you'll notice how flexible Italian can be while still being grammatically correct!
</CultureTip>
