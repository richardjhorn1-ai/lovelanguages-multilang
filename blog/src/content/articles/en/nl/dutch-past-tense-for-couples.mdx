Being able to talk about the past is essential for any couple. You want to reminisce about your first date, share stories from your childhood, or simply tell your partner what happened during your day. Dutch has two main past tenses, and mastering them will unlock a whole new dimension of conversation with your Dutch-speaking partner.

## The Two Dutch Past Tenses

Dutch uses two past tenses that you'll need to know:

1. **Perfect tense (voltooid tegenwoordige tijd)** - Used in everyday conversation
2. **Simple past (onvoltooid verleden tijd)** - Used more in writing and storytelling

Good news: In spoken Dutch, the perfect tense is used about 90% of the time. So that's where we'll focus.

## The Perfect Tense: Your Go-To Past Tense

The Dutch perfect tense is formed with a helping verb (hebben or zijn) plus a past participle. It's similar to English "I have worked" or "I have gone."

### The Formula

**Subject + hebben/zijn + past participle**

<VocabCard
  word="Ik heb je gemist"
  translation="I missed you / I have missed you"
  pronunciation="ik hep yuh khuh-MIST"
  example="Ik heb je zo gemist vandaag."
/>

<VocabCard
  word="We zijn naar Amsterdam geweest"
  translation="We went to Amsterdam / We have been to Amsterdam"
  pronunciation="vuh zine nahr AM-ster-dam khuh-WAYST"
  example="We zijn vorig jaar naar Amsterdam geweest."
/>

### Regular Past Participles

For regular verbs, form the past participle like this:

- Take the verb stem
- Add **ge-** at the beginning
- Add **-t** (if the stem ends in t, k, f, s, ch, p) or **-d** (all other cases)

| Infinitive | Stem | Past Participle | Example |
|-----------|------|-----------------|---------|
| werken (to work) | werk | gewerkt | Ik heb gewerkt |
| koken (to cook) | kook | gekookt | Jij hebt gekookt |
| leven (to live) | leef | geleefd | We hebben geleefd |
| horen (to hear) | hoor | gehoord | Ik heb het gehoord |

<CultureTip title="The 't Kofschip Rule" flag="ðŸ‡³ðŸ‡±">
Dutch learners use the mnemonic "'t kofschip" to remember which consonants take -t in the past participle. If the stem ends in t, k, f, s, ch, or p (the letters in 't kofschip), add -t. All other stems get -d. Your partner might smile when they hear you've learned this classic Dutch grammar trick!
</CultureTip>

## Hebben vs. Zijn: Which Helper Verb?

Most verbs use **hebben**, but verbs of motion or change of state use **zijn**.

### Verbs with Zijn (Important for Couples!)

## The Simple Past (For Storytelling)

While the perfect tense dominates conversation, the simple past (imperfectum) appears in stories and written Dutch. Here's a quick overview:

### Regular Verbs in Simple Past

Add **-te/-ten** (after t, k, f, s, ch, p) or **-de/-den** (other consonants) to the stem:

| Verb | Stem | I/He/She | We/They |
|------|------|----------|---------|
| werken | werk | werkte | werkten |
| leven | leef | leefde | leefden |
| horen | hoor | hoorde | hoorden |

### Common Irregular Simple Past Forms

| Infinitive | I/He/She | We/They |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| zijn (to be) | was | waren |
| hebben (to have) | had | hadden |
| gaan (to go) | ging | gingen |
| komen (to come) | kwam | kwamen |
| zien (to see) | zag | zagen |
